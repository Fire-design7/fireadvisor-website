Walk into almost any public building in Europe and you'll see the same green and red pictograms — regardless of country or language. That's no accident: they follow the **ISO 7010** standard, which defines unified graphical safety symbols that are recognizable without translation.

## How each sign is built

- **Shape and color** — defined by ISO 3864-1 (for example, a green square for evacuation, red for fire equipment).
- **The graphical symbol itself** — designed according to the principles of ISO 3864-3, so it's clear and unambiguous visually, without text.

## The most common signs in practice

- **Running man toward a door** — marks an evacuation exit or direction of travel.
- **Fire extinguisher** — marks the exact location of a portable fire extinguisher.
- **Fire hydrant / hose reel** — marks an internal fire hydrant.
- **Manual call point** — the location of a button to manually trigger the alarm.
- **Assembly point** — the location where evacuated occupants gather outside the building.

## Why standardization matters in practice

In an actual emergency, there's no time to read and interpret. Standardized symbols work precisely because they're recognized instantly, regardless of whether a visitor reads Cyrillic, Latin script, or doesn't speak the local language at all. That's why signage isn't an aesthetic choice — it's part of a building's overall safety system and needs to comply with the current revision of EN ISO 7010.

Sign placement isn't a standalone task — it's tied to the [evacuation plan](/en/uslugi/evakuatsionni-shemi) and the layout of the fire equipment itself.
